## Covering a faded old tattoo for an international female client

The client’s old tattoo had faded over time and no longer gave her a sense of satisfaction when she looked at it. Because it was located across the ribs and hip, the previous tattoo also strongly influenced how the new composition needed to be designed.

The client wanted to cover the old tattoo with a large, colorful piece capable of completely changing the appearance of the area.

Instead of hiding the old tattoo beneath one thick black shape, Lan Pink developed a layered design that included:

- the dragon’s head and body;
- scales;
- shaded areas;
- peony flowers;
- leaves and background details;
- red, pink, orange, and green color areas.

This combination created enough light and dark sections to manage the old tattoo while allowing the new artwork to retain depth, movement, and clear focal points.

## Why are dragons and peonies suitable for covering old tattoos?

Not every tattoo design is suitable for covering an existing tattoo.

A cover-up design needs a composition flexible enough for the artist to place darker areas exactly where they are needed. It must also include enough detail and sufficient scale so that the old tattoo no longer remains the main visual focus.

A dragon works particularly well for this purpose because many of its elements can be adjusted:

- the dragon’s body can curve around the old tattoo;
- scales provide multiple layers of detail;
- the dragon’s head can become a new focal point;
- black and grey areas help conceal visible old lines;
- the dragon’s belly and color transitions keep the piece from becoming overly dark.

Peonies and leaves are used to connect the remaining areas, soften the composition, and fill spaces that the dragon’s body does not pass through.

As a result, the new artwork does not look like an isolated patch placed over the old tattoo. The entire design becomes a complete tattoo with a unified theme and its own visual flow.

## Assessing the old tattoo before designing a cover-up

Before beginning an **old tattoo cover-up**, the artist needs to examine the area in person or review clear photographs of the tattoo.

Important factors include:

- the size of the old tattoo;
- whether the lines remain dark or have faded;
- the existing ink colors;
- the exact placement on the body;
- the condition of the skin;
- which areas can accommodate darker sections;
- how far the client is willing to extend the new design.

A faded tattoo often offers more flexibility than one that is still very dark. However, that does not mean any new design can simply be placed over it.

The new artwork still needs to be planned so that the most important parts of the existing tattoo sit beneath details with stronger covering ability, such as scales, dragon hair, leaves, darker flower petals, or shaded areas.

## A good cover-up is not simply about using more black ink

A common misconception is that covering an old tattoo only requires using a large amount of black ink.

In reality, making the entire area too dark can cause the new tattoo to lose detail and become a heavy block on the body.

In this dragon and peony tattoo, black and grey are used strategically where structure or stronger coverage is required. The remaining areas are balanced with red, pink, orange, and green tones.

This approach helps ensure that:

- areas requiring coverage have enough depth;
- the dragon’s head and body retain visible detail;
- the peonies do not disappear into the background;
- the artwork contains highlights and visual breathing room;
- the overall design better complements the curves of a woman’s body.

A successful cover-up does more than prevent viewers from noticing the previous tattoo. The new piece must also have a natural composition and be strong enough to stand on its own as a complete design.

## A dragon composition that follows the curves of the ribs

The rib area has a curved surface that changes considerably with body movement. When the design must also conceal an old tattoo, the composition requires even more careful planning.

In this artwork, the dragon’s head is placed higher on the body to establish a new focal point. Its body flows down the ribs and connects with clusters of flowers and leaves near the hip.

This arrangement directs the eye vertically along the body rather than toward the area where the old tattoo was located.

Lan Pink developed the composition so that:

- the dragon’s head acts as the primary focal point;
- the body creates vertical movement along the ribs;
- scales and darker shading help cover the old tattoo;
- peonies soften the overall appearance;
- leaves and background elements connect transitional areas;
- the colors are distributed so the design does not feel too heavy on one side.

When the client stands upright, the tattoo forms one continuous composition. When she turns or changes position, the dragon’s body and flowers continue to follow her natural curves.

## A dragon and peony tattoo for women

Dragons are commonly associated with strength, courage, and powerful energy. Peonies, by contrast, bring softness, fullness, and elegance.

This combination suited the client’s goal: the new tattoo needed enough character to completely change the feeling of the old piece while remaining harmonious with the female body.

The dragon serves as the center of the composition. Clusters of peonies balance its sharper lines and introduce additional color around the hip.

Because of this balance, the artwork does not feel entirely aggressive or dark. It remains strong while retaining softness, making it well suited to the rib placement and the client’s personal style.

## Red and pink tones give the old tattoo area a new appearance

One of the most distinctive features of the artwork is the group of red, pink, and orange tones used across the dragon and flower petals.

Black and grey maintain structure, create depth, and help conceal portions of the old tattoo that remain visible. Green leaves balance the warm colors and create clearer separation between the flowers, dragon, and background.

When planning colors for a large cover-up, Lan Pink does not look only at each individual detail. The artist also evaluates the entire area from a distance to determine:

- which areas are becoming too dark;
- where brighter colors are needed;
- whether the old tattoo still draws attention;
- whether the dragon’s head stands out enough;
- whether the flower clusters are balanced with the dragon’s body.

The goal is for viewers to first see a complete dragon and peony tattoo rather than search for the old tattoo beneath it.

## Working with detail across the ribs and hip

The ribs require significant concentration because the skin curves, stretches, and moves with every breath.

During a cover-up, the artist must also observe how the new ink interacts with skin that already contains pigment. The design needs to be completed section by section while the overall appearance is checked as the client changes position.

### The dragon’s head

The dragon’s head is the strongest visual focus of the artwork, containing concentrated details such as the eyes, horns, snout, and surrounding lines of movement.

This section draws attention upward and creates a new visual center for the entire tattoo.

### The dragon’s body and scales

The dragon’s body is adjusted to follow the curves of the client’s body and the area that needs to be covered. Layers of scales, the belly, and shaded sections provide depth while creating the detailed areas required to manage the old tattoo.

Rather than filling the entire body with the same level of darkness, each area is divided into light and dark sections so the dragon does not become flat or turn into one heavy block of color.

### Peonies and leaves

The peonies soften the dragon while helping extend the composition down toward the hip.

Their layered petals create new areas of color across the section being covered. Green leaves add contrast and connect the empty spaces between the dragon’s body and the flowers.

## Cover-up tattoos often need to be larger than the original design

When covering an old tattoo, the new design usually needs to extend beyond the original area.

If the new artwork is only the same size as the old tattoo, the artist has very little room to:

- adjust the composition;
- place highlights and shadows;
- create natural transitions;
- redirect the visual focus;
- conceal old outlines;
- develop a clearly defined new subject.

In this artwork, the design was extended from the ribs down toward the hip. The larger scale gave the dragon and peonies enough space to create a new story rather than function merely as a patch over the previous tattoo.

However, not every cover-up requires the same amount of expansion. The appropriate size depends on the darkness, colors, and placement of the previous tattoo.

## Can an old tattoo be covered completely?

The ability to conceal an old tattoo depends on the individual case.

Some tattoos that are faded, lightly lined, and relatively small are easier to manage. Tattoos with dense black ink or difficult placement may require a larger design, more dark sections, or additional treatment before a cover-up can be attempted.

The artist needs to examine the actual tattoo to assess:

- which areas can be covered effectively;
- where darker sections need to be placed;
- whether any details should be retained;
- how large the new design needs to be;
- which colors are suitable;
- what level of coverage can realistically be achieved.

For this reason, cover-up consultations should be based on clear photographs or an in-person assessment rather than simply choosing a new design and placing it over the old tattoo.

## A cover-up design must be adjusted to the client’s actual body

With cover-up tattoos, selecting an attractive design on a screen is not enough.

The artwork needs to be tested directly on the body to check:

- which new detail will sit over the old tattoo;
- the height and angle of the dragon’s head;
- the curve of the body;
- the position of the flowers;
- the distance from the waistline and clothing;
- the balance when viewed from the front and back;
- the amount of extension required.

This is especially important around the hip and ribs, where even a slight change in angle can affect both the coverage and the softness of the final composition.

Lan Pink adjusts each design to the client’s body shape and actual tattoo placement so the new artwork becomes a natural part of the body.

## When should you cover an old tattoo, and when should you keep it?

A cover-up may be suitable when an old tattoo:

- has faded or lost color;
- no longer has clear lines;
- no longer matches your preferences;
- has a composition you are unhappy with;
- sits in an area that can be developed into a larger artwork.

In some cases, parts of the old tattoo may still be usable. The artist may choose to retain certain details and connect them with the new design.

The goal does not always need to be covering everything with a large amount of ink. A good solution should be based on the actual condition of the tattoo and the wearer’s long-term expectations.

## Can the theme of a cover-up tattoo be changed?

Yes, absolutely.

A dragon and peony composition was selected for this project, but not every old tattoo needs to be covered with a dragon.

Depending on the existing tattoo, placement, and personal preferences, a new design may be developed around themes such as:

- flowers and leaves;
- snakes;
- animals;
- blackwork;
- ornamental tattooing;
- Asian-inspired patterns;
- abstract designs;
- a custom concept based on the client’s personal story.

The most important factor is that the new theme must have a structure suitable for the tattoo underneath. A design may be beautiful but still unsuitable for cover-up work if it contains too much open space or relies on lines that are too fine.
